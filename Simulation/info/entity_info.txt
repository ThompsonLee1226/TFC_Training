General
Each product and component has a fixed standard price, visible in the Information section. Sales and Purchasing roles cannot negotiate prices directly.

Instead, negotiation occurs through service level agreements (SLAs). A contract index is calculated based on SLA terms: Better SLA terms → Higher contract index → Higher sales price / Lower purchase cost.

The contract index acts as a multiplier to the base price, impacting final costs.

Finished product
Each product has a fixed basic sales price, which can be found in the Information section. However, the final sales price depends on the contract index, calculated based on the terms of the service level agreement (SLA) negotiated by the VP of Sales. A higher contract index reflects favorable SLA terms, leading to a higher sales price, while a lower index results in a discount.

For instance, a contract index of 1.05 means the customer pays 5% more than the basic price, whereas 0.95 indicates a 5% discount. However, the final price is determined not only by negotiations but also by the actual realization of the agreed SLA terms. If service commitments are not met, customers may demand penalties or discounts.

Raw materials
Like finished products, components also have a fixed basic purchase price visible in the Information section. The final purchase price is determined by the contract index, which depends on the terms negotiated by the VP of Purchasing.

Suppliers offering relaxed delivery terms may provide a lower contract index (e.g., 0.95 = 5% discount), whereas stricter delivery terms lead to a higher contract index (e.g., 1.05 = 5% premium).

While all suppliers offer the same basic price for a component, their starting contract index may differ, affecting the final purchase cost.

General
All entities in The Fresh Connection have unchangeable characteristics. For example, components have a fixed number of items per pallet, while suppliers are defined by location and transport costs. This section outlines these entities and their key characteristics.

Customers
The Fresh Connection serves various customers, including retailers and convenience stores, each with their own delivery requirements.

Finished product
All finished products have a fixed basic price, but the final sales price depends on customer satisfaction with supply chain performance. Good performance may result in a premium price, while poor performance leads to penalty discounts.

Packaging affects storage efficiency, as items fit into outer boxes, pallets, and layers. Products have a limited shelf life, and customers require delivery within a specified period to ensure they retain the product for a portion of its shelf life. If stock is held too long, it cannot be sold and must be discarded.

Bottling lines
Each bottling line has specific production capacities, including:

Liters bottled per hour

Changeover times

Breakdown sensitivity

Packaging material tolerances

Operator requirements

Depreciation and maintenance costs

Mixers
Mixing is constrained by technical capacity limits:

Minimum batch size (to ensure proper mixing)

Maximum capacity (maximum liters per batch)

Run time (time required to mix one batch)

Cleaning time (after each juice flavor is mixed)

Additionally, there are variable costs per mixing hour and fixed costs for depreciation and maintenance.

Raw materials
All components have a fixed basic price, but the actual purchase price depends on supplier negotiations. Stricter supply terms → Higher price. More flexible supply terms → Lower price

Packaging formats:

Large rolls → Delivered on pallets

Pulp & additives → Supplied in drums, IBCs (Intermediate Bulk Containers), or tank trucks

Drum capacity: 250 liters

IBC capacity: 1,000 liters

Tank truck capacity: 30,000 liters

Shelf Life Considerations:

Fruit pulp has a limited shelf life and must be used before expiration.

Storage beyond its usability in The Fresh Connection's tank yard or raw materials warehouse results in waste and financial loss.

The expiry countdown starts from the supplier’s order date.

Suppliers
Each supplier has unchangeable characteristics, including:

Location: Impacts transport costs (distant suppliers → higher costs).

Market share: Indicates supplier size and stability in the world market.

Certification: Certified suppliers are generally more reliable in delivery and material quality.

Packaging quality: Poor packaging leads to higher rejection rates and bottling line issues.

Lead time: The period between placing an order and receiving it.

Distant suppliers → Longer lead times

Make-To-Order (MTO) production → Longer production times than Make-To-Stock (MTS).

In-house pulp production → Longer lead times than traders.

Delivery reliability: The % of orders delivered within the agreed lead time. However, this is a promise, and suppliers may not always meet it due to:

Capacity constraints

Peak demand periods

Production inefficiencies

Free capacity: Determines how much additional production a supplier can handle.

Gross capacity: Total production capacity before contracting with The Fresh Connection.

Net capacity: Remaining capacity after fulfilling The Fresh Connection’s contract.

Low net capacity → Higher risk of late deliveries, especially during peak demand.

General
The Fresh Connection (TFC) computational model converts team decisions into logistical results and individual and team performance scores. While the exact simulation model is not detailed, key interpretation aspects will be covered.

Simulation
To reduce statistical uncertainty, each round is simulated for 20 years by running 40 iterations of six-month periods. This ensures that team results remain comparable, even when different decisions are made.

Key benefits of this approach:

More accurate logistics data: Numbers like consignments received are averaged over 40 simulations, avoiding misleading whole-number fluctuations.

Reliable graphical reports: Charts and figures reflect consistent patterns, reducing discrepancies between visual data and actual calculations.

By using this method, The Fresh Connection ensures fair comparisons and realistic insights across different teams.

Customer demand
Customer demand is a critical factor in the simulation model. While base demand parameters are identical for all teams to ensure comparability, each team can influence demand patterns through negotiations with customers.

Key influences on demand:

Sales Volume Rebates: Offering rebates can make demand more volatile and impact purchase behavior.

Promotional Pressure: Increased promotional efforts typically boost demand.

To support stock replenishment, The Fresh Connection uses a weekly forecast model based on demand patterns. While teams cannot adjust the baseline forecast, they can enhance accuracy by negotiating agreements with customers and improving promotional forecasts.

Delivery
If The Fresh Connection cannot deliver an order line in full, it remains on backorder and will be delivered once stock is available. However, delayed fulfillment lowers the realized service level, which can result in penalties or revenue loss.

Shelf life
The model tracks when each finished product is produced. Shelf life starts immediately, and products nearing expiry are prioritized for delivery. Stock that exceeds shelf life and cannot be sold is written off as obsolete.

Production planning
Each team generates a weekly production plan, aiming to spread production orders evenly to minimize capacity peaks. However, bottlenecks like limited production capacity and component shortages may disrupt plan adherence.

Component demand
Component demand is based on the Bill of Materials and production planning. The system forecasts demand for each component, and if stock runs low, it automatically triggers a replenishment order from the chosen supplier.

Supply
Suppliers differ in reliability, impacting on-time delivery. Unreliable suppliers may deliver late, leading to uncertainty in supply chains, whereas reliable suppliers consistently deliver on time.
